Final Report


Abstract

Everyday twenty-two people die waiting for a transplant. Around 65% of organs are rejected by centers while 10% of accepted organs are not used in transplants. Rejections occur due to low confidence from doctors about compatibility, time spent waiting for decisions being made, and human error. This $34B industry is missing many chances at saving lives and money. UNOS stores data for the thousands of transplants that occur and current medical charts have hundreds of data points on patients. This seemed like a perfect opportunity to let machines solve this problem.

Method and Results

 

Processing

The dataset obtained contained 15000 unidentifiable kidney transplant information. Most preprocessing of the data was done by the source and we further processed it using data_preprocessing.py. The dataset contained demographic information such as donor age, weight, ethnicity, in addition to clinical data such as dialysis history. The data was processed by the source to the shape of 1 dimension, so each transplant included 26 numerical values for each category. It should be noted the raw data had information in more complicated shapes (e.g. dialysis shape (3,2)) and in the future, a neural network might find trends in the more complicated data.

 

The success/failure of a transplant is a complicated statistic. Follow ups are done in the months and years after a transplant and rejection can happen at any time. Transplantâ€™s may fail due to complications, unrelated illnesses, or surgical side effects. A common method of determining transplant success in kidney transplants is by grant failure. Graft failure is a significant complication following allogeneic hematopoietic cell transplantation (AHCT) that leads to rejection of the organ. We determined the transplant a success if a grant failure did not occur within 1 year. This is our gf value in Appendix A.
